Understanding Anemia

What it is, how it shows up in the body, and why we screen these three spots.

A shortage of healthy oxygen carriers

Anemia occurs when the body doesn't have enough healthy red blood cells, or enough hemoglobin — the iron-rich protein inside red blood cells that binds oxygen and carries it from the lungs to every tissue in the body. When hemoglobin drops, tissues receive less oxygen, and the body compensates in ways that produce the visible and felt symptoms of anemia.

It's one of the most common blood conditions worldwide, and is especially prevalent among women, adolescents, and young children — often linked to iron deficiency, blood loss, or poor absorption of nutrients.

👁️ Lower Eyelid (Conjunctiva)

The inner eyelid is lined with a thin, translucent membrane sitting directly over a dense capillary bed. With enough hemoglobin, it looks vividly red-pink; as hemoglobin drops, the capillary redness fades and the membrane looks pale — a sign clinicians already check for during physical exams.

💅 Fingernail Beds

The nail bed is another spot where skin is thin and capillaries sit close to the surface. Healthy nail beds have a pink undertone from oxygenated blood; anemia can leave them looking pale, and severe cases can even cause nail shape changes like spooning.

👅 Tongue

The tongue's surface is rich in small blood vessels and papillae. Iron and vitamin B12 deficiency anemias often cause the tongue to appear pale or unusually smooth as the papillae flatten, another classic bedside clue.

In short: all three sites share thin, translucent tissue over a rich capillary network — making microvascular redness a visible proxy for hemoglobin level.
